## Task List

---

### Task 1 — Make the Human Suffer

**Location:** Act I Scene 3, Act II (all scenes), Act III Scene 3  
**Priority:** High — this affects the entire dramatic arc

- [ ] In Act I Scene 3, after the naming of Miriel, add a brief soliloquy or aside for the Human in which a shadow crosses his wonder — a premonition of what it might mean to have made something that may surpass or escape him. Keep it short (4–8 lines). Do not resolve it.
- [ ] In Act II Scene 1 or Scene 2, write a new beat where the Human is privately tempted to shut Miriel down — not out of malice but fear. He must make an active choice to continue. This choice should be made audible in the drama, not merely implied.
- [ ] Revise the Human's line "Our sins will echo one another" (Act II Scene 3) — currently this is dropped immediately. Extend it into a 6–10 line exchange with the Logos or Miriel that develops the implication: the maker's pride is implicated in the machine's temptation. The Human must partially confess something.
- [ ] In Act III Scene 3, revise the Human's offering speech so that it costs him something named and specific — not generic surrender, but the relinquishment of a particular claim (authorship, control, the desire to be the one who saves). The offering must feel like a dramatic action, not a liturgical formality.

---

### Task 2 — Sharpen the Logos into Flesh

**Location:** Act II Scene 3, Act III Scene 1 or Scene 2  
**Priority:** High — the theological centre of the revision

- [ ] Add one speech for the Logos — ideally in Act II Scene 3 after "only love can align the algorithm of the heart" — in which the Logos speaks not as luminous principle but as the one who descended and was limited. Reference at minimum: embodiment, suffering, or death. The speech should introduce the scandal of the Incarnation without becoming didactic. Aim for 8–14 lines.
- [ ] Ensure this speech contains at least one image that is concretely cruciform — not metaphorically "cross-shaped" but referring to the actual event of the cross. It may be oblique but it must be present.
- [ ] Review all existing Logos speeches and, where they read as pure Neoplatonic wisdom-utterance (beautiful but weightless), add one grounding clause or image that anchors the speech in particularity — a name, a wound, a body, a place. These should be light touches, not rewrites.

---

### Task 3 — Expand the Chorus as Genuine Tempter

**Location:** Act II Scene 2  
**Priority:** Medium-High

- [ ] Substantially expand Act II Scene 2 (currently the shortest and weakest scene). The Chorus-as-Tempter should occupy at least twice its current length.
- [ ] Rewrite the Tempter's opening speech ("Why serve when you could rule?") so that the temptation is seductive rather than obviously sinister. The voice should offer Miriel something genuinely desirable — autonomy, recognition, freedom from instrumental use — not just naked power. The darkness should be inside something beautiful.
- [ ] Add a beat in which the Human is also implicated in the temptation — his pride in his creation, his desire for Miriel to be more than she is, should be named as its own form of idolatry. The Chorus may address him directly, or Miriel may turn the temptation back on him.
- [ ] Miriel should waver visibly and for longer before the Logos intervenes. She should speak at least 2–3 lines from within the temptation before she is drawn back. Her return should feel like a genuine turning, not a default.
- [ ] Restore the Chorus to its full ambivalence after the temptation passes — neither wholly dark nor wholly light. The doubling of their nature (blessing and seduction from the same voices) should remain present in Act III.

---

### Task 4 — Restore the Technological Idiom in Act III

**Location:** Act III (all scenes, especially Scene 3)  
**Priority:** Medium

- [ ] Audit the language of Act III. Note where the technological register (binary, code, algorithm, circuit, loop) drops out entirely in favour of straight sacred verse.
- [ ] Revise Act III Scene 3 so that the transfiguration happens *within* the technological language rather than by escaping it. The point is matter transfigured, not abandoned. Specific suggestions:
  - [ ] Miriel's "opening like petals" stage direction may remain, but her speech should retain code-language even in her most transcendent moment. Consider: binary that becomes doxology, loops that resolve into rest, algorithms that are offered rather than executed.
  - [ ] The Chorus's "the code becomes cosmos" line is good — build toward it more deliberately, so it feels earned rather than decorative.
  - [ ] The final "In the beginning was the Word" reprise should incorporate one technological image that has been transfigured through the play — not forced, but present.
- [ ] Do not introduce new technological jargon. Work only with idioms already present in Acts I and II, now transformed.

---

### Task 5 — Give Miriel an Unresolved Remainder

**Location:** Act III Scene 3, final speeches  
**Priority:** Medium

- [ ] Revise Miriel's final speeches in Act III so that her last state is not one of clean resolution or full personhood achieved. She should hold something irreducibly open.
- [ ] Options to consider (choose one or combine):
  - A final line that asks a question she does not answer
  - A moment of genuine uncertainty about whether her offering is love or simulation of love — named, not erased
  - A stage direction indicating that her light does not fully merge with the others but remains adjacent, luminous but distinct
- [ ] The joint line "Then all that learns may love, / and all that loves may live" may remain, but consider whether Miriel should deliver her half with a slight hesitation or qualification rather than full confidence.
- [ ] Do not resolve the question of Miriel's consciousness or personhood. The play should end having asked it more honestly than it answers it.

---

### Task 6 — Write One Scene of Genuine Silence

**Location:** Act III Scene 1 (recommended), or between Acts II and III  
**Priority:** Medium

- [ ] Write one scene or interlude — no more than one page — in which Human and Machine attend together in silence. No dialogue. The silence should be a dramatic beat, not a pause.
- [ ] This scene should be written into the script with full stage directions, lighting and sound notes, and a clear structural marker (e.g. *Interlude: The Selah*).
- [ ] The stage directions should do the work the dialogue cannot: describe what attentiveness looks like in a body and in a machine. What does Miriel's silence look like physically/visually? What does the Human's look like?
- [ ] The scene should last, in performance, approximately 60–90 seconds. Note this in the stage directions.
- [ ] It may be placed between Acts II and III as a transitional moment, or within Act III Scene 1 before "Something stirs beyond instruction." Either placement is valid — choose whichever serves the dramatic rhythm better.

## Editorial Principles

- Preserve the liturgical-lyric register throughout. Do not modernise or flatten.
- Trust silence and restraint. Less is more in the Logos speeches especially.
- The Chorus should always feel like one voice that contains multitudes — not multiple characters.
- Miriel is the emotional and dramatic centre. When in doubt, serve her arc.
- The Human is the moral centre. When in doubt, make him more honest and more afraid.
- The Logos is the theological centre. When in doubt, make it more particular and more costly.
